Taj Mahal

An Eternal Testament to Love & Beauty

01   Overview

What is the Taj Mahal?

The Taj Mahal is one of the most magnificent structures ever built by human hands — an immense mausoleum crafted entirely from white Makrana marble, shimmering ivory in sunlight and pale gold at dusk. It stands as a complete architectural complex: a grand gateway, formal gardens, a mosque, a guesthouse, and the tomb itself, all arranged in perfect symmetry along a single north–south axis.

Commissioned in 1632 CE by the Mughal Emperor Shah Jahan, the monument was built to house the tomb of his beloved wife, Mumtaz Mahal, who died during the birth of their fourteenth child. Shah Jahan's grief was immeasurable, and he channelled it into the creation of the world's most celebrated monument to love. The name Taj Mahal is believed to derive from "Mumtaz Mahal," meaning Crown of the Palace.

02   Location & History

Where & When Was It Built?

The Taj Mahal sits on the southern bank of the Yamuna River in Agra, Uttar Pradesh, in northern India — approximately 200 kilometres south-east of New Delhi. Construction began around 1632 CE and was largely completed by 1648–1653 CE, spanning over two decades of continuous effort. The project drew on the talents of over 20,000 artisans, craftsmen, and labourers recruited from across the Mughal Empire, Persia, Ottoman Turkey, and Europe.

The principal architect is widely credited as Ustad Ahmad Lahouri, a Persian master builder attached to the Mughal court. The complex's design draws deeply from Persian, Islamic, and earlier Mughal architectural traditions, synthesising them into something entirely unprecedented. Semi-precious stones — lapis lazuli, jade, crystal, turquoise, and carnelian — were inlaid into the marble using the parchin kari technique, creating the glittering floral and calligraphic panels that adorn the tomb's interior and exterior.

03   Significance

Why Is It Historically Important?

The Taj Mahal represents the apex of Mughal architecture — the cultural and artistic high-water mark of one of history's most powerful empires. It stands not only as a monument to personal grief but as a symbol of the Mughal Empire's unparalleled wealth, administrative strength, and cosmopolitan artistic vision at its zenith under Shah Jahan's reign (1628–1658 CE).

In 1983, UNESCO inscribed the Taj Mahal as a World Heritage Site, describing it as "the jewel of Muslim art in India and one of the universally admired masterpieces of the world's heritage." It was also declared one of the New Seven Wonders of the World in 2007, following a global poll of over 100 million votes. Today it draws approximately six to eight million visitors annually, making it India's most visited monument and a cornerstone of its cultural identity.

04   Quick Facts

Fascinating Facts About the Taj Mahal

  • The entire structure weighs an estimated 74,000 tonnes — yet the four surrounding minarets are built slightly tilted outward, so that in the event of an earthquake, they would fall away from the main tomb.
  • The white marble changes colour throughout the day — it appears pinkish at dawn, dazzling white at noon, and takes on a golden hue by moonlight, symbolising different moods of a woman.
  • Construction required material from across Asia: marble from Makrana (Rajasthan), jasper from Punjab, jade & crystal from China, turquoise from Tibet, and sapphire from Sri Lanka.
  • The main dome stands 73 metres (240 feet) tall — roughly the height of a 20-storey building — and is surrounded by four smaller domed kiosks called chattris.
  • Shah Jahan himself was later imprisoned by his own son Aurangzeb and spent his final years gazing at the Taj Mahal from the Agra Fort across the river.
  • The gardens follow the charbagh (four-garden) layout, a Quranic symbol of Paradise divided into four quadrants by water channels, with the tomb centred at the intersection.
  • The calligraphic inscriptions on the gateway are designed with letters that increase in size the higher they go, creating an optical illusion of uniform height when viewed from the ground.
